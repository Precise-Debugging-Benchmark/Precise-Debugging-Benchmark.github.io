Precise Debugging Benchmark: Is Your Model Debugging or Regenerating?

Abstract

Unlike code completion, debugging requires localizing faults and applying targeted edits. We observe that frontier LLMs often regenerate correct but over-edited solutions during debugging. To evaluate how far LLMs are from precise debugging, we introduce the Precise Debugging Benchmarking (PDB) framework, an automatic pipeline that converts any coding dataset into a debugging benchmark with precision-aware evaluation.

PDB generates buggy programs by synthesizing verified atomic bugs and composing them into multi-bug programs. We further define two novel metrics, edit-level precision and bug-level recall, which provide fine-grained measurements of how many necessary edits are made and how many bugs are resolved.

We release PDB-HARD, a 5,734-example evaluation set for measuring debugging precision and recall. Experiments show that frontier models, such as GPT-5.1-Codex and DeepSeek-V3.2-Thinking, achieve unit-test pass rates above 78% but exhibit precision below 45%, even when explicitly instructed to perform minimal debugging. Finally, we show that iterative and agentic debugging strategies do not substantially improve precision or recall, highlighting the need to rethink post-training pipelines for coding models.

PDB-HARD Dataset

We construct PDB-HARD, an evaluation set of 5,734 examples using the PDB generation and evaluation pipeline. The dataset sources tasks from two existing coding benchmarks: BigCodeBench, which focuses on API usage, and LiveCodeBench, which emphasizes algorithmic reasoning.

Results

Experiments on PDB-HARD reveal behaviors that unit tests fail to capture. Frontier models exhibit strikingly different rankings under edit-level evaluation. Models such as GPT-5.1-Codex and DeepSeek-V3.2-Thinking achieve high unit-test pass rates (>78%) but low edit precision (<45%), while Qwen3-Coder-480B attains comparatively lower unit-test pass rates (71%) yet substantially higher precision (66%). Ablation Studies

Freeform vs. minimal debugging. We compare model performance under freeform prompting (debugging freely without restrictions) versus minimal-debug prompting (explicitly instructed to make minimal edits). Across all models, freeform prompting results in substantial drops in precision and recall. Even strong models like Claude-Sonnet-4.5 achieve less than 60% precision under freeform prompting, while GPT-5.1-Codex fails to reach 20% precision. These results demonstrate that prompt-level constraints are necessary but insufficient to induce precise debugging behavior.

Comparison of model performance under minimal-debug and freeform prompting.

Iterative and agentic debugging. Both iterative (up to three revision attempts) and agentic settings (with unit-test feedback) improve unit-test scores and recall, but precision remains unchanged or degrades. This suggests that iterative interaction improves correctness by expanding the scope of modifications rather than by refining edits toward minimal repairs.
